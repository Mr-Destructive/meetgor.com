b := 10
a, b = b, a</code></code></pre><p>This is pretty handy, I encountered this while trying to solve the advent of code problems. I thought can we do this in Golang as I have lazy habits from Python?</p><ul><li><p><a href="https://pkg.go.dev/slices#Delete" rel="nofollow ugc noopener">Slices.Delete</a><span> in Golang is Wired</span></p><p>I mean just look at this</p></li></ul><pre><code><code>s := []int{1,2,3}
slices.Delete(s, 1, 2)
fmt.Println(s)
// [1, 3, 3]

s = []int{1,2,3}
s = slices.Delete(s, 1, 2)
fmt.Println(s)
// [1,3]
</code></code></pre><p>I mean, If the function is returning the modified slice, why are we mutating the original one? Do one or the other, not both.</p><p>The safer route will be like this then:</p><pre><code><code>s = []int{1,2,3}
newS := slices.Delete(slices.Clone(s), 1, 2)

fmt.Println(s)
// [1, 2, 3]

fmt.Println(newS)
// [1,3]</code></code></pre><p><span>I need to dive deep into why this is the way it is.
